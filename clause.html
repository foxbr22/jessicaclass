Relative Clauses

Part 1: Understanding Relative Clauses

Relative clauses are subordinate clauses that provide additional information about a noun or pronoun in the main clause. They are introduced by relative pronouns (who, whom, whose, which, that).

Relative Pronouns Reference Table

Relative Pronoun Use For Function Example
who People Subject The woman who lives next door is a doctor.
whom People Object The man whom we met yesterday is her brother.
that People & Things Subject or Object
(defining clauses only)		 The car that I bought is red.
whose People & Things Possession The student whose paper won received a prize.
which Things & Animals Subject or Object The book which I read was interesting.

Important Notes:

  • "That" can replace "who," "whom," and "which" in defining relative clauses only
  • "That" cannot replace "whose" - there is no possessive form of "that"
  • "That" cannot be used in non-defining relative clauses (clauses with commas)
  • In informal spoken English, "who" is often used instead of "whom"
  • In informal English, the relative pronoun is sometimes omitted when it's the object of the verb (e.g., "The book I read" instead of "The book that/which I read")

Rule 1: Defining Relative Clauses (Restrictive)

Defining relative clauses provide essential information that identifies which person or thing we are talking about. They are not separated by commas.

1. The book that I borrowed from the library is overdue.

2. The woman who called earlier wants to speak to you.

Rule 2: Non-defining Relative Clauses (Non-restrictive)

Non-defining relative clauses provide additional, non-essential information and are separated by commas. We cannot use "that" in non-defining clauses.

1. My brother, who lives in Australia, is visiting next month.

2. Paris, which is the capital of France, is famous for its architecture.

Rule 3: "Who" for People

Use "who" to refer to people (as the subject of the relative clause).

1. The teacher who teaches math is very popular.

2. Anyone who wants to join should sign up by Friday.

Rule 4: "Whom" for People (Objects)

Use "whom" to refer to people when they are the object of the verb in the relative clause. "Who" is often used in spoken English instead of "whom".

1. The candidate whom we selected declined the offer.

2. The students whom you met yesterday are in my class.

Rule 5: "Which" for Things

Use "which" to refer to things and animals (in both defining and non-defining clauses).

1. The car which broke down has been repaired.

2. The movie, which won several awards, was based on a true story.

Rule 6: "That" for People and Things (Defining Only)

Use "that" to refer to both people and things in defining relative clauses only. "That" cannot be used in non-defining clauses.

1. The phone that I bought last week is already broken.

2. The people that work here are very friendly.

Rule 7: "Whose" for Possession

Use "whose" to show possession for both people and things.

1. The student whose paper won the competition received a scholarship.

2. I live in a building whose roof was damaged in the storm.
